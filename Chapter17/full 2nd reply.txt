You are right to push architecture into the first phase. In fact, if this is intended as a learning exercise, starting with a clean structure will make every later change easier to reason about and far less error-prone. Your current implementation mixes UI and logic in a way that works for a simple demo, but it will quickly become fragile once you add things like decimals, backspace, or scientific functions. Moving to a ViewModel-based architecture early, with a clear separation between UI, state, and calculation logic, is the most valuable “phase 1” investment you can make.

I would not recommend doing everything in a single pass. Even though it might feel efficient, it significantly increases the risk of introducing bugs and makes it harder to verify correctness at each step. Since your requirement is that the app must remain runnable and error-free in every phase, a staged approach is the better choice. However, I do agree with your instinct to redefine the phases so that architecture is not treated as an afterthought. Instead, phase 1 should establish the structural foundation and include a few small, low-risk functional improvements so that the refactor feels meaningful and testable.

A sensible phase 1 would therefore focus on introducing a ViewModel, moving all state and input handling out of the Composables, and isolating the calculation logic into its own component. Alongside that, you can safely add decimal support, a backspace function, and improve the button layout using weights instead of fixed sizes. These changes are relatively contained, do not require a full rewrite of your calculation engine, and give immediate user-facing improvements while validating your new architecture.

Since you are not interested in the more advanced expression parsing and history features, it makes sense to skip that middle layer entirely. Instead, your second phase can focus on extending capability within your chosen constraints. Adding sin, cos, tan, and square root directly to the existing keypad is a reasonable design decision, especially for a learning project. It avoids the complexity of multiple modes while still introducing more advanced operations. The key consideration here will be how your calculation logic evolves to support these functions without becoming a tangle of special cases. Because you established a clean architecture in phase 1, you will be able to extend the calculation engine in a controlled way.

In summary, the best approach is a two-phase plan rather than a one-shot implementation. Phase 1 should combine architectural refactoring with small, safe feature improvements so that the app remains stable and demonstrably better. Phase 2 can then build on that foundation by expanding functionality, including the scientific operations you are interested in. This approach keeps the application runnable at every step, reinforces good engineering practices for your readers, and avoids the cognitive overload and instability that often come from trying to change everything at once.
